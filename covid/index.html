On the ongoing COVID pandemic

Last updated: August 13, 2022 leaning on sources organized and shared by Amy Hua

“It is time – long past time – to give up on the lazy fantasy that we can let it become ‘endemic’ and ‘uncontrolled’ because it inconveniences us, … or because the virus will magically evolve to some ‘mild’ state.” (Ian Ricksecker, at the end of a long but recommended informative thread)

As the latest omicron variant continues to spread, I want to push back on the idea that “everyone will get COVID eventually” and the societal return to pre-COVID behaviors without basic precautions. Here are some resources on why:

Nearly 1 in 5 American adults who have had COVID-19 still have “long COVID”. 1 in 13 have “long COVID” symptoms. Another study in the Netherlands (Aug 2022) shows that 1 in 8 COVID patients experience lingering symptoms.

If these statistics still don’t seem high, keep in mind that the recent subvariants are the most immune-evasive we’ve seen, meaning reinfection risk is much higher and potentially challenges the effectiveness of existing vaccines, and absolute risk of those experiencing “long COVID” remains high. (Eric Topol)

(Also yes, for those of us who have caught COVID at least once before: every reinfection leaves you more vulnerable to the next!)

Recent language (e.g. from the CDC) describes recent cases of COVID as “mild”, but “mild” and asymptomatic cases of “long COVID” consist of long-term conditions that dramatically reduce quality of life. These include not just respiratory but also cognitive, cardiovascular, and autoimmune (every organ is susceptible. In other words, long COVID can be disabling.

Some of the most common symptoms include fatigue, brain fog, short-term memory loss, breathlessness, changes in heart rate and blood pressure, all of which have already been harming economies (NPR (US), Financial Times (UK), Bloomberg).

A tsunami of disability is coming as a result of ‘long COVID’.

Additionally, consistently about half of all long COVID patients contract symptoms consistent with ME/CFS - “an untreatable, disabling, lifelong condition that renders many homebound and even bedbound.”

Bottom line, long COVID is debilitating, and recent increased risk of (re)infections make it crucial for us to continue putting in serious effort to avoid infection and spreading infection, both for ourselves and for others.

So what? The least I hope you get out of reading this is that this encourages you to mask up more, test regularly where possible, and to exercise more caution with regard to social events and crowded areas.

I get it and I’ve been there - the peer pressure and appealing comfort to go mask-free is hard. But let’s recognize this as an ableist mindset. These basic precautions are collective behaviors that can keep our community safe. It’s not only the particularly at-risk populations who will benefit from your precautions, but also any of us who still hope to live a full life without suffering the persistent effects of long COVID.

“The rush to ‘normalcy’ papers over the reality that we are not going back to a world before COVID. Like everyone else, I am nostalgic for that world. But I also don’t want to live in a society that thinks it is okay to sacrifice the vulnerable for its own comfort. There is a difference between the science-backed pursuit of restoring our lives to their fullest possible meaning—the joy of spontaneity—and lying to ourselves that COVID-19 is “just the flu.” We want our old life; we need to build our new one.” The Atlantic
